# Screenplay Craft Reference

_Extracted from Final Draft's Complete Screenwriting Guide — April 2026_
_Source: [finaldraft.com/learn](https://www.finaldraft.com/learn/how-to-write-a-screenplay-complete-guide)_

## 1. Formatting Fundamentals

### Scene Headings (Slug Lines)
- Format: `INT./EXT. LOCATION — TIME OF DAY`
- Be specific: micro location before macro (`INT. BATHROOM, KEVIN'S HOUSE — MORNING`)
- Time of day options: DAY, NIGHT, MORNING, AFTERNOON, EVENING
- Every scene starts with one

### Action Lines
- Short and punchy — **4 lines or fewer per block**
- Use ALL CAPS sparingly for important props, sounds, and character movements
- Use active tense, not passive ("Joe SLAMS the door" not "The door was slammed by Joe")
- Break up exciting action into short, staccato paragraphs for cinematic pacing
- Avoid "orphans" — single words dangling on their own line

### Character Cues
- Character name in ALL CAPS above dialogue, centered
- First appearance: name in ALL CAPS with brief, revealing description
- Describe character by behavior/trait, not just appearance ("refuses to give way when she walks" > "blonde and pretty")

### Dialogue
- Indented, near center of page
- `(CONT'D)` when dialogue continues after action or page break
- `(O.S.)` for off-screen, `(V.O.)` for voice over

### Parentheticals
- Use sparingly — between character cue and dialogue
- Only when delivery isn't clear from context, or to indicate who they're talking to
- Don't over-direct actors

### Transitions
- Largely passé in modern scripts — the scene heading IS the cut
- Use sparingly and purposefully: `SMASH CUT TO`, `FLASH CUT TO`, `MATCH CUT`, `DISSOLVE TO`
- `CUT TO` is implied between every scene — only include for emphasis
- `JUMP CUT` — two shots of same scene with slight variation, intentionally jarring
- `INTERCUT` — primarily for phone calls or parallel action
- `FADE IN` / `FADE OUT` — relic but acceptable bookends

### Page Rules
- Courier 12pt font (industry standard, non-negotiable)
- 1 page ≈ 1 minute of screen time
- Features: 90–120 pages (90 min most common)
- Shorts: proportional to runtime target

## 2. Structure

### Three-Act Structure
| Act | Pages (Feature) | Purpose |
|------|-----------------|---------|
| Act I | ~25-30 | Setup: protagonist, world, inciting incident |
| Act II | ~30-75 | Confrontation: obstacles, rising action, midpoint |
| Act III | ~75-120 | Resolution: climax, denouement |

### Key Structural Beats
- **Inciting incident**: by page 15 at latest
- **Act I turning point**: propels us into Act II
- **Midpoint**: power dynamic shifts (ascending party falls, falling party rises)
- **Act II turning point**: triggers Act III
- **Climax**: the big resolution
- **Denouement**: wrap loose ends

### Cause and Effect Structuring
- Every beat should cause the next: "This happened, **therefore**..." or "This happened, **but**..."
- Keeps characters in constant peril/conflict
- Stronger connective tissue between beats = more engaging story

### For Shorts (5-7 min / ~5-7 pages)
- Condense to simple inciting incident → escalating complications → resolution
- One complex element: complex structure OR complex characters (not both)
- Must stand alone and come to satisfying conclusion

## 3. Character

### Character Introduction
- First appearance = opportunity to tell the reader something you can't directly film
- Bad: "ALICIA (20s), blonde and pretty"
- Good: "ERICA (20s), blonde, refuses to give way when she walks"
- Reveal character through behavior, not exposition

### Character Biography (Pre-writing Exercise)
- Birth to story start: childhood, family, formative events
- Professional, personal, private life in present
- "Day in the life" — what do they do from waking to sleeping?
- How characters feel about and relate to each other
- Even if it never appears in the script, it informs every line

### Character Arc
- How is the protagonist different at beginning vs end?
- Track emotional through-line: how they feel at every story beat
- Arc should reflect or express the script's theme

## 4. Dialogue

### Voice
- Your unique worldview expressed through characters
- Characters should sound distinct from each other — not all surrogates for the writer
- Read dialogue aloud — does it roll off the tongue?

### Rules
1. **Show don't tell** — don't have characters describe what would be more compelling to watch
2. **Avoid "on the nose"** — characters stating exactly what's on their mind without subtext
3. **Differentiate voices** — each character should sound unique
4. **Control cursing** — not everyone curses; reflect reality
5. **Conflict > harmony** — arguments reveal character differences organically
6. **Don't repeat known info** — if audience already knows it, skip it
7. **Minimize exposition** — spread it out, keep it conversational, make it entertaining

### Exposition Best Practices
- **No info dumps** — don't blurt backstory through therapy sessions, news reports, or title cards
- **Don't front-load** — space exposition throughout the script (indirect exposition)
- **Keep it conversational** — people reveal themselves gradually, not in monologues
- **Make it entertaining** — Tarantino buries exposition inside entertaining banter
- **Don't over-explain** — audiences fill in blanks when emotionally invested

### Naturalistic Dialogue
- Listen to real people — how they speak in different situations (boss vs friend)
- Real conversations are messy, awkward, interrupted
- People dance around truths, mask feelings, use subtext
- Working-class characters can be direct without being expository if it's true to character

## 5. Flashbacks & Montages

### Flashbacks
- Must keep the story moving — add information we wouldn't otherwise get
- Have a purpose and raise the stakes
- Keep as short as possible
- Clearly indicate beginning and end
- Keep formatting consistent across the screenplay
- Can embed directly in action for quick, self-contained flashbacks

### Montages
- Ellipse time to pack information efficiently
- Simple: `MONTAGE of Jessie in various date outfits.`
- Complex: indicate BEGINNING and END in ALL CAPS, use slug lines for different locations
- Must serve the story, not just fill time

## 6. The First 10 Pages

Executives decide whether they like your project in the first 10 pages (often sooner).

### Must Accomplish
1. **Introduce characters** — with revealing behavioral traits, not just physical description
2. **Build world** — narrow, specific construct that facilitates your story; dress it up to stand out
3. **Make it easy to understand** — by page 10, reader should know what the story is about
4. **Make it engaging** — do people care? Do they want to keep reading?
5. **Inciting incident** — should have already happened by page 10-15

### Emotional Connection
- As long as people connect emotionally with your lead, they'll keep turning the page
- Dynamic first 10 pages that feel true to the story = mark of a great writer

## 7. Loglines

### Formula
- Protagonist + Inciting Incident + Action/Stakes
- 1-2 sentences maximum
- Describe character by trait, not name ("A listless teenager" not "Napoleon")

### Rules
- Present tense
- No questions (that's a tagline)
- No "this is the story of..."
- No run-on sentences
- Mention time period only if important

### Examples
- **Speed**: "A young police officer must prevent a bomb exploding aboard a city bus by keeping its speed above 50 mph."
- **Napoleon Dynamite**: "A listless and alienated teenager decides to help his new friend win the class presidency in their small western high school, while he must deal with his bizarre family life back home."

## 8. Theme

- The engine that drives the story and gives it meaning
- Rarely stated explicitly — lurks beneath the surface
- Protagonist's arc usually reflects/expresses the theme
- When stuck, theme tells you where to go

### Common Themes
- Vengeance / Redemption
- Good / Evil
- Love / Hate
- Coming of Age / Death
- Sacrifice, Family, Transformation

---

## 9. Rewriting

1. **Take a break** — set the draft aside for perspective (weeks to months)
2. **Gather diverse notes** — from people whose taste you trust, look for intersecting feedback
3. **Outline changes first** — fix structural problems in outline before rewriting 120 pages
4. **Know which notes to take** — look at "the note behind the note"
5. **Be prepared for collaboration** — bigger projects = more writers involved
6. **Do a dialogue pass** — read all dialogue critically, ensure each character sounds distinct

---

## 10. Comedy-Specific Notes

- Comedies are culturally focused — can limit appeal
- Hard to set up without big names attached
- Small, offbeat comedies (Napoleon Dynamite, Rushmore) can break through but are hard to pull off
- Humor can be expressed through: witty banter, one-liners, situational comedy, character voice
- Even non-comedies benefit from humor — action/horror/thriller films increasingly contain humorous dialogue
