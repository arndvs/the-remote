# THE REMOTE — Beat Breakdown

## The Gas Station

_Commute sequence — Beat 3 of 3_

---

## WHAT HAPPENS

Dave is driving to work. He already knows about the fuel gauge — spotted it at the start of the commute, right after the radio came on. The whistle he'd been unconsciously matching stopped mid-phrase. He said "yeah" to nobody. Kept driving. That was two coffee denials ago.

The fuel gauge is past empty. The warning light has been on since yesterday — long enough that it's started to feel decorative. The needle is below the line and not coming back. This is a man who kept meaning to stop for gas and kept not doing it.

A gas station approaches on the right.

He begins to slow.

The PUSH hums — different from its baseline. A shift in pitch and quality. Not concern. Not worry. Impatience. _We don't have time for this. We have somewhere to be._ Push Mode does not have a gas station stop in it. Push Mode goes forward.

Dave looks at the gas station. Looks at the road ahead. Weighs it.

The bed hums again. Slightly more pointed.

Dave straightens up. Drives past the gas station.

The bed resumes its baseline hum — not relieved, not warm. Satisfied. _Correct. We're moving._

A beat of normal driving.

The car sputters. Coughs. Dies.

Dave coasts to the shoulder. Stops. Sits in the dead car in complete silence.

The bed hums. Baseline. Patient. The destination has not changed.

A beat. Dave sits there. Then he opens the door, gets out, and walks to the back of the car. He doesn't need to be told. He looks at the car. Looks at the road ahead. Puts his hands on the trunk.

Dave starts pushing.

The PUSH rolls behind him, pushing Dave pushing the car, down the shoulder of the road.

**CUT TO:**

The office parking lot. Dave arrives, disheveled — shirt slightly untucked, tie askew, worse than before. The PUSH behind him, utterly unruffled, exactly as it was when the day started.

What happened between those two shots is not shown. We don't know if they pushed to the next gas station, or all the way to the office, or something else entirely. The parking lot tells us everything we need to know: they got here. It took a while. Something happened out there.

Dave straightens his tie slightly. Still crooked. He walks toward the building.

---

## THE REFRAME — WHAT THE BED IS ACTUALLY WARNING HIM ABOUT

The bed's hum when Dave slows toward the gas station is easy to misread as concern — _"hey, you need gas."_ But then the bed hums again more pointedly, and Dave drives past the station, and the bed settles back into its satisfied baseline.

The bed was never warning Dave about the fuel gauge. It was warning him not to stop. Push Mode does not stop. There are no gas station provisions in Push Mode. The bed's only investment in this moment is the schedule. Forward. Office. That's it.

When the car dies anyway — the direct consequence of the bed's insistence that they not stop for gas — the bed does not recalibrate, does not express anything resembling guilt or acknowledgment. It hums. It nudges Dave out of the car. The car's mechanical failures are entirely outside its jurisdiction. Forward is forward. The car is Dave's problem.

This is a darker and funnier reading than the bed looking out for Dave. The bed made this situation worse. It did so in service of the schedule. It has no regrets.

---

## THE FUEL GAUGE — DAVE'S ACCUMULATED POOR CHOICES

The light has been on since yesterday. Dave noticed it at the start of the commute — the Ludacris whistle dying mid-phrase is the first time in the film the audience sees what's coming before it arrives. He acknowledged the problem. He did not address it. Two beats later, the gas station gives him his chance. The bed takes it from him.

This is the slow accumulation of a man who keeps meaning to deal with things and keeps not dealing with them.

The same man who texted _"in"_ at 5pm. The same man who had to be walked to work by a bed. The same man who looked at himself in a bar bathroom mirror and said _"you're doing great"_ with complete conviction.

The fuel gauge is not a plot point. It is a character point. Dave's car has been running on fumes since yesterday and this morning — hungover, being pushed to work by a bed, having already spilled coffee on himself — is the morning it finally runs out. This is what Dave's life looks like from the outside. The bed sees it clearly. It doesn't care. Forward.

---

## THE CUT — WHY WE DON'T SHOW WHAT HAPPENS

Dave pushing his car, the PUSH pushing Dave, highway shoulder, destination unknown.

Cut.

Parking lot. Dave disheveled. PUSH unruffled.

The gap between those two shots is where the comedy lives. The audience fills it in and whatever they imagine is funnier than anything we could show. Did they push to the next gas station a quarter mile away? Did they push the whole commute? Is the car in the lot or still on the highway? Did someone help? Did no one help?

We don't know. Dave doesn't explain. Nobody asks. The tie is worse. Something happened out there. Moving on.

This is consistent with the film's entire approach to consequences: they happen, they are absorbed, life continues, logistics are not explained.

---

## THE WARNING HUM — SOUND DESIGN

The bed's warning hum is a subtle but crucial sound design moment. It needs to communicate impatience without losing the bed's fundamental character of professional patience.

The shift: slightly higher pitch than baseline, a quality of attention rather than rest — the difference between a machine idling and a machine that has noticed something it doesn't like. It should read as: _I see what you're thinking. Don't._

It lasts approximately two seconds the first time. If Dave continues slowing, it returns — slightly more pointed, slightly shorter. Then Dave drives past the station and it settles back to baseline.

Mixed correctly, the audience should feel the first hum slightly before they consciously register what Dave is looking at. The hum and the gas gauge land at the same moment.

**What the hum is NOT:** It is not alarm. It is not concern for Dave's wellbeing. It is not the sound of a machine noticing a problem. It is the sound of a machine that has a schedule and does not appreciate detours.

---

## THE PUSH PUSHING DAVE PUSHING THE CAR

The image of Dave pushing his car while the PUSH pushes him is the beat's visual payoff — and it extends the film's central joke into new territory.

All day the PUSH has been pushing Dave through his life. Now Dave is pushing something too. The chain: PUSH → Dave → car. Three things in a line on a highway shoulder, moving forward because that is the only option.

Dave is passive in this moment even while physically pushing — he's not solving the problem, he's not thinking, he's just moving forward because the bed is moving forward and the car is between them. He has been so thoroughly processed by the morning that pushing a car down a highway in office clothes while being pushed by his bed is just the situation. He adapts. He always adapts.

This shot should be wide. The highway around them. The scale of it. A man in office clothes, slightly untucked shirt, bad tie, hands on the trunk of a dead car, a PUSH by RISE™ rolling behind him. Hold it just long enough to land. Then cut.

---

## CONTINUITY NOTE

Dave arrives at the office without explaining anything. His state — more disheveled than he was, tie worse, possibly a light sweat — tells the story without telling it.

An employee heading in from the other direction passes him. Doesn't break stride. Dave arrives looking wrecked; the bed arrives looking pristine. Nobody reacts. Marcus sees him later at his desk (Scene 14) — that's the payoff. One nod. "'Sup." They move on.

The car is never mentioned again.
